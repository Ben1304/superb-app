🧠 Managing stress at work
Mental health · tap to read
Stress in the workplace is something almost everyone experiences at some point, and recognising it early makes a real difference. Common signs include difficulty concentrating, feeling irritable or overwhelmed, disturbed sleep, and a sense of dread about going to work. None of these mean you are failing — they mean your body is telling you something needs to change. The most effective thing you can do is identify your main stressors and, where possible, address them directly. This might mean having an honest conversation with your manager about workload, blocking out focused time in your calendar, or simply learning to say no to things that are not a priority. Small changes to how you structure your day can dramatically reduce the pressure you feel. It also helps to build recovery into your routine rather than treating it as a reward for finishing everything. Taking a proper lunch break, stepping outside for ten minutes, and finishing work at a consistent time are not luxuries — they are what keep you performing well over the long term. Trying to push through without rest tends to make everything take longer and feel harder. If stress is persistent or beginning to affect your health, please reach out to your EAP provider. Talking to someone trained to help is one of the most effective things you can do, and everything you share is confidential.
🪑 Correct sitting posture
Physical health · tap to read
Most back, neck, and shoulder pain in office workers comes down to how they sit — and the good news is that with a few simple adjustments, it is largely preventable. The goal is a position where your body is supported without being strained in any direction. Start with your chair. Your feet should rest flat on the floor with your knees at roughly a 90-degree angle. If you cannot achieve this, a footrest helps. Your lower back should be supported — either by the chair's lumbar support or a small cushion placed at the curve of your spine. Sitting with your lower back unsupported for hours at a time is one of the leading causes of chronic back pain. Your screen should be at eye level so you are not tilting your head up or down. If you use a laptop, consider a stand and a separate keyboard. Your elbows should be close to your body and bent at roughly 90 degrees when typing. Keep your wrists straight rather than bent upwards or downwards. The most important thing, however, is not to stay in any one position for too long. Set a reminder to stand up and move for a few minutes every 30 to 45 minutes. Even a short walk to make a drink or a few gentle stretches at your desk will make a significant difference over time.
😴 Sleep and performance
Recovery · tap to read
Sleep is the single most powerful performance tool available to you, and it is free. Most adults need between seven and nine hours a night, and consistently getting less than that affects concentration, decision-making, mood, and immune function — often before you even notice it is happening. Research consistently shows that people who are sleep-deprived significantly overestimate how well they are functioning. The quality of your sleep matters as much as the quantity. A consistent bedtime and wake time — even at weekends — helps regulate your body clock and makes it easier to fall asleep and wake up naturally. Avoiding screens in the hour before bed makes a measurable difference, as the blue light they emit suppresses the melatonin your body needs to wind down. Caffeine has a half-life of around five to six hours, meaning a coffee at 3pm still has half its effect at 8pm or 9pm. If you struggle to fall asleep, cutting off caffeine after midday is worth trying before anything else. Alcohol, while it can make you feel drowsy, significantly disrupts sleep quality in the second half of the night and often leaves you feeling less rested than if you had not had it at all. If you are consistently waking in the night or struggling to fall asleep despite good habits, it is worth speaking to your GP. Sleep disorders are common and very treatable, and addressing them can transform how you feel at work and in general.
🏃 Staying active at a desk job
Physical health · tap to read
Sitting for extended periods has been linked to a range of health issues including back pain, poor circulation, and increased risk of cardiovascular disease — and this holds true even for people who exercise regularly outside of work. The key is not just to exercise more, but to move more throughout the day. The simplest change you can make is to treat movement as a default rather than an exception. Take calls standing up or walking if you can. Use the stairs. Walk to a colleague rather than sending a message. These small decisions add up considerably over a working week without requiring any additional time or effort. If you work from home, it is easy to go hours without moving at all. Setting an alarm every 45 minutes as a prompt to stand up, stretch, or walk around the house for a couple of minutes is one of the most effective habits you can build. Some people find a standing desk useful, though the evidence suggests alternating between sitting and standing rather than standing all day. In terms of structured exercise, even 20 to 30 minutes of moderate activity — a brisk walk, a cycle, a short gym session — most days of the week has significant benefits for both physical and mental health. You do not need to be training for anything. Consistency matters far more than intensity, and something is always better than nothing.
🔥 Recognising burnout
Mental health · tap to read
Burnout is a state of chronic exhaustion caused by prolonged stress, and it is more serious than simply feeling tired or having a difficult week. The World Health Organisation recognises it as an occupational phenomenon, and it tends to develop gradually over months rather than appearing suddenly. The three core signs are exhaustion — a deep, persistent fatigue that rest does not seem to fix; cynicism — a growing detachment from your work and the people around you, sometimes accompanied by feelings of resentment; and reduced effectiveness — a sense that you are putting in the effort but producing less, or that tasks that used to feel manageable now feel impossible. Burnout does not mean you are weak or that you chose the wrong career. It usually means you have been giving more than you have been able to recover for too long. Common contributing factors include excessive workload, lack of control over how you work, unclear expectations, poor relationships at work, and a mismatch between your values and what the job requires of you. If you recognise these signs in yourself, the most important thing is not to ignore them. Speak to your manager or your EAP provider. Taking time to recover early is far more effective — and far less disruptive to your life — than waiting until you have nothing left. Burnout that goes unaddressed can take months to recover from fully.
🤝 Building positive workplace relationships
Wellbeing · tap to read
The quality of your relationships at work has a significant impact on how much you enjoy your job and how well you perform. People who feel connected to their colleagues report higher job satisfaction, are more likely to ask for help when they need it, and are generally more resilient when things get difficult. Building good workplace relationships does not require being particularly social or outgoing. It is mostly about consistency and genuine interest. Remembering small details about colleagues, following up on things they have mentioned, and being reliable when you commit to something all contribute to trust over time. So does being honest — people generally prefer a colleague who tells them the truth over one who always agrees with them. Conflict is inevitable in any working environment, and how you handle it matters more than whether it happens at all. When disagreements arise, try to address them directly and early rather than letting them fester. Focus on the issue rather than the person, and approach the conversation with curiosity rather than defensiveness. Most workplace tensions are rooted in misunderstandings or differing priorities rather than bad intentions. If you are working remotely or in a hybrid arrangement, relationships require a bit more intentional effort. Making time for non-work conversations, contributing to team channels, and being present on video calls rather than multitasking all help maintain the connection that comes naturally in a shared physical space.
